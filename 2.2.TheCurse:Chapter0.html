The Cave Of The Dead

The Cave Of The Dead The Curse The Curse: Chapter 0 The Curse: Origins
“Look, Lloyd,” it was apparent from his mannerisms that he was getting tired of the rather ‘unemployed’ man barging into his office every other day in search of a day job, “I’ve asked in every firm around town, there are no offers going on.”
“Roberts, I get it, it’s just that, things are going to be even tougher than usual now with-,” he paused, trying to glance at the employer to see if he shared any sympathy, “-and, rent’s getting higher by three cents starting from next quarter, I need something.” “Lloyd, I’ve told you many times, I have one principle in life, and that is when the going gets tougher, the tough gets going. SO, toughen up.”

It had been around four months now that Lloyd had been visiting employers around town asking them to provide him with adequate jobs that would help him earn a few more than what his book brouth in, even though they were regarded as massive cults in central Massachusetts.

He returned home, with a hole in his jacket’s pocket, and one in the living room table which the rats turned into a slide, peeping out to fetch a few pieces of the fruits kept by the handwoven, Persian imported basket, which too had a hole forming. He sat at his table, and opened the light-pink book before him and started writing, and then he scribbled on the words, and then he wrote some more, and then scribbled on them too. An idea just wouldn’t form in his mind. He pushed the chair back, and got up, pouring himself a coffee made from the beans he had picked up in the Amazon when he went on an expedition, to visit a mansion he deemed possessed the king’s soul. He stood by the window still, staring at a bird that had landed there, pecking its beak on a worm, ripping out its tiny guts and then swallowing it whole. Surprising how downright cruel Mother Nature is.

The sun reached the horizon. Lloyd and his fellow, Pole, were now travelling down Count Dormenshier road with a pair of rifles in their hands, distributed equally, along with a carriage pulled by two lean Morgan horses, pulling a small wooden bench for laying the corpses on. Hunting was a very common hobby in this side of the town.

“Tell me again, P, how’d you get your name as Pole,” Lloyd asked after a minute of struggling to form sentences from wheezing and laughing.
Pole let out a sigh. “How many times Lloyd. How many times to repeat it? The count backed into a pole which was constructed by my father. After a very thorough disagreement, the count’s daughter, my mother, was married off to the constructor. For this turned his wood into jewels, my father decided to name me Pole.”
“The man’s mental. Anyways, see any bucks down here?”
“I did last week. Don’t know where they went.”
“Oh!” Lloyd signalled Pole to be quiet, his eyes fixated on a golden-brown deer hopping around the thorny hedges. Lloyd took aim, and shot, sending ripples through the leaves of the trees nearby. A loud thud of the deer’s carcass hitting the ground sent the birds flying out.

“We’re eatin’ good tonight, P!”
“You betcha. Felt like one hell of a buck.”
The two approached the buck who lay beside a shallow pond, fed by a cascading stream from the rocks nearby, accompanied with lush vegetation around it. They mustered the courage to pick up the buck as they approached it, as a precautionary measure, for which they were thoroughly surprised to see there was no deer there. The courage in question was now a lingering doubt.

Something glided through the hedges behind Lloyd, a shadow that embodied a wild buck. Lloyd followed it, his astonished gaze fixated on it as it entered into a dark opening and disappeared.

“There!” Pole gave out a shout, “The buck!”
“What if it’s a trick of an old bugger or some?”
“You’re mental, Lloyd.”

It wasn’t much of an agreed decision in going into the cave, but it would be worth a shot. The opening had odd symbols engraved in poppy-ink, with the darkness engulfing all the strangeness that the cave held within.

As they walked into the twists and turns of the cave, Lloyd felt that there was one more accompanying them in this unnatural adventure.

“Right then, shall we turn ar-?”
“Don’t be a wuss,” came the reply of a weirdly brave Pole to the confused fellow’s question. If the turns hadn’t been strange, the walk was also accompanied by strange screams—multiple of them, to which there were turned ears, but zipped mouths.

They reached a sort of opening. The walls lined with odors of dead rats, at the end of which, a pedestal stood tall, with a chest on its head. The content being the auspicious matter, Pole went ahead and opened the chest. What followed was clouds, grey and thin, and the screams of the two men.

Their visions having been rendered blurred, the two made a run for their horses, kicking them and hitting them so as to get them moving, like amateur Polo players.

Lloyd’s mind wandered a thousand places, but it was great that he had a friend with him. His mind filtered out every noise in the forest, adamant on getting him home.

The village greeted them with lit lamps. The two made way for their houses, both standing tall in their white disguises. Lloyd sat by an oil-lit lamp and started his entry.

‘A wild-buck hunting trip saw us enter into estranged lands of a certain cave by the outskirts of the forest. An ordinary cave showed us at the end a rather bizarre chest, within which contained smoke, which was greeted to us by Pole’s negligence (with whom I decided to accompany along in this dinner fiesta, as some would call it in the plantations).’

He closed the book,opening it to enter the date in, and then closed it again, when suddenly a thud came from the front door. It was Glenn, a fellow neighbour of Pole who had a distressed look painted on his face.

“Pole’s not back yet!”
This shout of help by Glenn alarmed the already half-alarmed senses of Lloyd. It would’ve been the most stupid to claim this, but could it be? His smoked vision did not take the time to clear until he had reached home.

After a dozen minutes of thinking, Lloyd set out for the outskirts. To his astonishment, Pole’s horse was there, now dead, with its throat slit open by a wild animal. Lloyd quickly made his way into the cave. In his hand he held a torch, which guided him into the cave.

He reached the chest, nothing there. No sign of life. He turned back to meet in front of him his friend crouched into the floor, as if he was waiting for lightning to hit him. Lloyd approached angrily, to meet with nothing. In the far end of the tunnel was Pole, now running into the dark light. Lloyd followed, reaching outside to feel the gushing wind, and seeing the shadowy figure move toward the village, he took off on his horse.

He reached the village, and having given up on the wild goose hunt, he quietly entered his house, and drew the curtains for sleep. It would be foolish to go out now, he thought, having seen the absolutely vile image of the slit horse.

But, no! He had to go back. It was a friend that’s in danger. He must have ran to the forest out of fright. He stood up straight. Picking up the rifle, a lamp and a waistcoat, he walked out toward the forest.

If he dies, so be it. But, he will find his friend. But what if he couldn’t. What if he’s… dead? No! Lloyd’s mind refused to go there. He mustered up courage, walking the bends in the forest.

He stopped, trying to make sense of a low humming sound in the perimeter. He looked around. Behind him, the bushes rustled. He looked the way he came. His footsteps had disappeared, and instead, streaks of blood appeared. He walked backwards, slowly, slowly.

A twig cracked, and his body descended twenty feet into the ground. He panicked, looking around to see skulls and bones poking at his face. He scrambled for balance. The night sky got covered. He looked up. Above him stood around thirty corpses, all looking down at him. All screaming. He closed his ears, but he could still hear them. The words, ‘YOU ARE NEXT,’ echoing through his mind.

He opened his eyes. Neither was it night, nor was he being haunted deep into the earth. He quickly gathered his belongings. The rifle, a cracked lamp was all there was to guide him.

He got home. After a momentary scare of a cat’s meow, he made his way to Glenn’s on his way to meet with Roberts. On hearing that Pole was still missing, Lloyd advised him in notifying the sheriff and staying indoors, the latter of which was met with fright and peculiarity.

The meeting with Roberts went as one would expect, with neither party helping the other. Lloyd was now adamant on finding what in God’s name was going on with Pole, and why he had been exposed to the violent image of corpses screaming in a vile voice.

He decided to stop in a county shop which was filled with Indian imported items of jaggery, spices like cloves and cardamom, and even French hats. He looked in finding himself a very sturdy crucifix, which when he overheard two lads talking about a,
“Spaniard, up in Washington. Drove out the curse from that house. ‘Abolisher of Witchcraftery’ they call him.”
Lloyd paid no attention to the comments then, for he was fixed on forgetting the wretched image off of his mind. After purchasing a baggy hat, Lloyd left the market, also due to the reason that he couldn’t stand the shopkeeper's British accent.

He returned home, and seeing there was nothing to do now, he quickly went off to sleep, skipping his daily evening rosewater drink. He closed his eyes, hoping - praying even - that yesterday was only a dream. Where was Pole? He couldn't be bothered with it at the moment. Was he a corpse, or those hellish creatures he saw the night before? No! Pole’s still out there. He has to be!

He drifted off to sleep.

Thinking of nothing…

Nothing at all…

He felt something breathing down on him. Another dream? He opened his eyes, to be met with around a dozen corpses standing opposite his bed, all pointing at him. All with their mouths open, screaming into the open abyss that was now his body, “YOU ARE NEXT!”

Lloyd ran from the house, narrowly making his way to his horse and then kicking it to go to the station, where he purchased a cent’s worth of ticket to Washington to meet with the Spaniard, for he was cursed.

He managed to pick up a satchel during his escape, where, after rummaging through, he found a half eaten roll, and his journal where he noted down from the last few weeks. He opened it, and took a pen and he started writing. ‘I’m off on my way to Washington. I heard of a Spaniard in the mist, able to strip me off of this curse. Yes, a curse.’

He drifted to sleep, which he deemed was impossible.

The sun struck its fierce golden rays into the cart. Lloyd got off at the station, where he hailed a carriage into driving him somewhere along the country to the Spaniard, which was done easier than he thought.

He saw him, pale and delicate Lloyd felt as he saw the man. About 30 or 40 in age. He told him everything that he experienced. He told him how he was cursed. The man heard everything with inviting ears, and then assured him he would catch the next train together and travel to the village.

The evening came on, and the two rode on the same train to the centre of the curse. Lloyd wrote down the events in his journal.

The man, to which he referred to as ‘psychic’, had with him a bag, with the Bible, a pack of five candles, a stake, and a crucifix. Must this man have confronted that Count Dracula he had heard of? It was also interesting to hear of all the ghosts he had helped get rid of, and that his way of dealing would end in a very complicated manner which would expel the spirits into a burst of energy, and that a ring of holiness was created around the wretched place.

They reached, and waited for the night to come upon them to enter the cave. Night finally came. They entered the cave, through its unending twists and turns. They saw the chest, but no sign of the souls or Pole. The man was thankfully patient in this strange ordeal. They were about to leave after a minute or two when they experienced an Earthquake. The man sat down, and drew a sort of pentagon on the floor. Placing the crucifix at the centre of the pentagon.

O spiritus impie! Utinam tu, in nomine Sancti Patris et discipulorum eius, ex hac spelunca exeas et in Sacra Oblatione Penez navigare exulares.

The man shouted this with a loud thrice. Suddenly, before the two, there appeared the corpses, screaming, as if they were getting burned. But, it only felt like they were getting burned. Their wretched faces filled the eyes of the two, as they came closer. Lloyd glanced at the psychic, who had his eyes closed, still repeating the line. The corpses came at arm’s length. Inching closer and closer until it almost touched Lloyd…

Almost.

He opened his shut eyes, and so did the psychic. To their surprise, the corpses were gone.

But, the uneasy feeling of being hunted was still present there. And it was obvious what the uneasiness was when they looked around the cave. Standing at least 10-feet tall was a strange creature. An unholy, hellish and demonic creature that now stared at them with red eyes. Its flesh looked as if it had been burned by fire, with marks all over the body that seemed to tell that the skin had been torn and stitiched back poorly. Its face resembled a reindeer, fixed on a terrible expression. Skulls popped out of its antlers, which still seemed to be screaming in terror. It stood on all fours, and was inching closer to the two.

Lloyd was now shivering in fright, but the psychic stood confident. They stared on, when suddenly the earth split into two and the creature fell into the earth, the fire eating him whole. The cave had been now filled with cracks, with withering roses growing out of it. They quickly exited the cave. Lloyd was now relieved, but also scarred.

He returned home. relieved to see the house stand tall, ignoring all past strangeness after bidding the man goodbye. For a strange reason, he couldn’t shake the feeling of a sort of strangeness in the man’s face. It was as if he was scared… of something terrible.

‘I had shamefully closed my eyes throughout both train rides, shameful for the way I snored, in the manner of sleep, which I could not obtain ever since my meeting with the spirit. Therefore, I had, after receiving news about a psychic living in a far-away town, left home to consult this psychic.

‘I reached the said psychic on the dawn of the 2nd day. My consultation with him had reignited my fear in this ghost, or spirit. The psychic then left his home with me back here in search of the spirit, which was a successful and an eventful journey. On the stroke of midnight the next day, I had followed the psychic to the abandoned cave, which served as an opening for 18th century oil drillers. On reaching a mile into the cave, we encountered paranormal activities, and saw the wretched spirits in front of us. The psychic had then cast a spell on the spirit and in turn assisted me to live peacefully'.

That night, he slept peacefully. And so did the night after that. And the night then. But, he never woke up, for he was trapped in the labyrinth of mazes, spanning into his days which he counted as three. The spirits still roamed within him, preventing him from moving. He saw the demon stand before him, its mouth breathing out a reddish gas, and a sort of mantra repeated in his head, which said, “Non sum finis, sum quod manet cum illa longe abest.”

In his nightmarish dreams, he saw gruesome killings, of women and children and men, all dropped in a pit of fire, screaming for help. A gold chimed, which resembled a God. A laugh emerged—a king’s laugh. He was next. Both arms of his were picked up, and then taken forward to meet a hellish pit of fire with coals cackling at the base. He was dropped…

He shot up, a common pattern in his morning routine every day now, and sat on the chair which paired the table with the journal. He was back home. But, he felt missing.
It wasn’t him sitting. It wasn’t him.

A sort of calling would painfully strike his brain in the form of a sharp knife which had ended it all, with his head thudding into the table and blood falling onto the journal. The curse was lifted, but with a cost.

People later found the journal, always closing it before the last page due to them being forewarned of danger. Some say the spirits killed him in a way. Some say this was a mean of escaping through torture.
After a span of a century, there are still theorists present, asking one thing to themselves (for they themselves are the only ones not bored of such atrocities), how would this connect to the conspiracy that would happen four decades later? But, first of all, does it even connect, for there is nothing, no books, articles on the matter, probably due to the scare that witchcraftery gave the people of those days.

Some brave souls attempted, back in those days, in opening the last day of the journal, all of them were dead the next day. Some say the words still have an effect now, others decline such claims.

The cave was finally put to an end by the Spaniard himself, the details of which were largely boring. The horror linger in these stories, and the horror is still not over...
